REAL VOICES

Personal stories from the D/HH community

Jenn Stringer, 40s

Berkeley, California

When Jenn Stringer was diagnosed with breast cancer in 2020, she faced her treatment alone — not by choice, but because pandemic restrictions prevented her husband from accompanying her to medical appointments.

As the chief information officer at the University of California, Berkeley, Stringer is hard of hearing and has always relied on having someone with her during important medical consultations. Medical professionals routinely advise patients to bring a companion when receiving serious health news — you need another set of ears to process difficult information.

But COVID-19 eliminated that safety net. Hospital visitor restrictions meant she navigated cancer treatment without her primary communication support. The irony wasn't lost on her: at the moment when clear communication mattered most, when she needed to understand complex treatment options and make life-altering decisions, she was entirely on her own.

"They always tell you to bring somebody in with a doctor when you're getting bad health news, right? Because you need somebody else to hear. And that was completely unavailable because of COVID."

Her experience highlights a broader crisis that emerged during the pandemic: policies designed to protect public health often failed to account for people who communicate differently, creating new barriers precisely when access to accurate medical information was most critical.

Shaylee Mansfield, 11

Austin, Texas

About a month into shelter-in-place orders, Shaylee Mansfield — an 11-year-old deaf actress in Austin — posted a video that resonated across social media, earning thousands of likes and retweets.

In the video, she signed her frustration while watching Instagram videos from her favorite creators.

"I don't understand my favorite people on Instagram. Why? No captioning!"

Her simple, direct message captured a fundamental injustice: while the pandemic pushed everyone online, social media platforms hadn't prioritized accessibility. Content that hearing people consumed effortlessly remained inaccessible to deaf users.

Shaylee's mother, Sheena McFeely — who is also deaf — explained that the pandemic's reliance on video chatting made navigating social and work life far more difficult for D/HH individuals. The shift to digital communication should have leveled the playing field, but instead it highlighted existing accessibility gaps.

McFeely emphasized that information is a powerful tool — it feeds knowledge, starts conversations, and connects people. Without access to that information, how can anyone learn and thrive?

For Shaylee, the lack of captions means she can't equally enjoy content with her hearing sister, Ivy. Birthday parties require interpreters. The "normal" life that hearing children experience — from casual social media browsing to mundane gatherings — remains frustratingly out of reach.

Shaylee's viral video was more than a complaint; it was a call to action from a generation that refuses to accept exclusion as inevitable.

Ashley Benton & Jorge Aristizabal

North Carolina & Seattle

For tens of thousands of deaf-blind Americans, the pandemic's emphasis on social distancing created an impossible paradox: the very measures designed to keep people safe threatened their primary means of connection and survival.

Ashley Benton, the deaf-blind services coordinator for North Carolina, explained the crisis succinctly: their entire culture and communication system relies on touch.

"Our way of communicating and our culture, everything relies on touch. Now we're not allowed to touch, and we have to practice social distancing."

Deaf-blind Americans navigate the world through touch — hand-over-hand signing for communication, fingers scanning braille signs for mobility, hugs and handshakes to feel connected. While not everyone in the community experiences complete darkness and total silence, advocates stress that touch is critical. During COVID-19, it also became a significant risk for contagion.

Jorge Aristizabal, a deaf-blind man living in Seattle, highlighted another danger: the six-foot distancing requirement wasn't just inconvenient — it was unsafe.

"The requirement to stay 6 feet away from other people is actually not safe for me. As a blind person, I need to touch my guide."

The pandemic exposed how public health guidance, while well-intentioned, failed to account for people who experience the world differently. For the deaf-blind community, following safety protocols meant sacrificing the very tools they need to navigate daily life safely.

Their experience demands a fundamental question: whose safety are we prioritizing when we design public health responses?
